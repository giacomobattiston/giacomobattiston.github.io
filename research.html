Research

Rescue on Stage: Border Enforcement and Public Attention in the Mediterranean

Migrants attempt to cross the Mediterranean Sea to reach Europe. European border enforcement (BE) has the conflicting objectives of preventing irregular migration and grant- ing humane treatment to migrants. The former would lead policy to increase the risk for migrants to cross in order to deter migration; the latter would require reducing this risk. Indeed, exploiting geo-located data on the universe of rescue operations between 2014 and 2017, I show that rescue policy influences departures. Further, I show that policy impacts risk for migrants, too, using exogenous variation. The implied trade-off is dynamic, as present policy influences future policy expectations by migrants. In addition, if the policy-maker is more concerned with outcomes during periods of higher monitoring, this trade-off interacts with public attention. I find empirically that this is the case, again using shocks to attention uncorrelated with policy and migration. Then, I propose a dynamic model of policy choice and public attention and estimate it. I show that the model can explain both long-term and short-term policy shifts.

Informing Risky Migration: Evidence from a Randomized Control Trial in the Republic of Guinea

Joint with Lucia Corno and Eliana La Ferrara

We study whether migration decisions towards Europe can be shaped by the provision of information related to the risk and cost of the journey and to the economic situation in the destination countries. We do so by conducting a randomized experiment among potential migrants in Guinea combining hard data, video-testimonies by migrants who completed the journey, and example stories of migration. We study the effect of three treatments: one in which we only deliver information about risks and costs, one in which we only give information about migrants' economic outcomes, and a treatment pooling the two previous types of information. Third Party Interest, Resource Value, and the Likelihood of Conflict

Joint with Matteo Bizzarri and Riccardo Franceschin

We build a model of resource war to investigate the impact of a change in the resource value on the likelihood of a conflict. A predator decides if to wage war against a resource holder and seize its resource. A powerful third party can intervene to back the defendant. However, it does not act as a social planner but it maximizes its own profits. Then, the effect of a change in resource value is a priori unclear. On the one hand, increased resource value results in a higher incentive to predate, on the other hand it makes for a higher incentive to intervene by the third party, increasing deterrence. Under general assumptions, we find that the probability of a conflict as a function resource value is is hump-shaped. We find empirical grounding for our model using data on interstate and civil conflicts from the MID and PRIO databases, instrumenting resource value with data on sediments from the CRUST database. In work in progress, we are developing a structural version of the model to study quantitatively counterfactual scenarios.
